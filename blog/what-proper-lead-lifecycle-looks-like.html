What a Proper Lead Lifecycle Should Look Like

March 2025  ·  7 min read

Most B2B companies have some version of a lead lifecycle. There's a form, a score, a threshold, and a handoff to sales. It exists. But in most cases, it's incomplete — and the gaps are where pipeline leaks.

A proper lead lifecycle isn't just about capturing and qualifying leads. It's about managing every stage of a buyer's progression from first contact to closed deal — with clear definitions, clear ownership, and clear actions at every step.

The Stages That Matter

Inquiry

Someone has taken an action — filled out a form, downloaded content, attended a webinar, clicked a high-intent ad. They're in the system. At this stage, you know they exist but you don't know much else. The goal here is not to call them. It's to gather more information and begin qualifying.

Marketing Qualified Lead (MQL)

An MQL is someone who has demonstrated enough interest and fits enough of your ICP criteria to warrant marketing follow-up. This is where most companies draw their first line — but the definition of "enough" is often where things break down. If the bar is too low, sales gets flooded with leads that aren't ready. If it's too high, real opportunities get delayed.

MQL criteria should be built from data about what your actual customers looked like before they converted — not from assumptions about what interest looks like.

Sales Accepted Lead (SAL)

This is the stage that most lifecycle designs skip entirely. An SAL is an MQL that sales has reviewed and accepted for follow-up. It creates accountability in both directions: marketing is responsible for delivering leads that meet the agreed criteria, and sales is responsible for acting on them within a defined timeframe.

Without this stage, there's no mechanism to identify when the marketing-to-sales handoff is breaking down.

Sales Qualified Lead (SQL) / Opportunity

Once a rep has connected with a lead and confirmed there's a real buying situation — budget, authority, need, and timeline — it becomes a qualified opportunity. This is the stage where marketing's work has fully converted into sales territory.

Recycled / Nurture

Not every lead converts on the first pass. Some aren't ready yet. Some have timing issues. Some need more information. A proper lifecycle has a defined path back into nurture for these leads — not a dead end where they sit uncontacted until someone remembers they exist.

Recycling leads back into marketing with context about why they didn't advance is one of the highest-leverage improvements most companies can make.

What Makes a Lifecycle Actually Work

A lifecycle isn't a flowchart on a slide. It's a set of agreed definitions, rules, and actions that everyone follows consistently.
  • Shared definitions — marketing and sales must agree on what each stage means and what criteria advance a lead from one stage to the next
  • SLA at each stage — how long can a lead sit in each stage before an action is required?
  • Feedback loops — when a lead is rejected by sales or fails to convert, that information needs to flow back to marketing so scoring and targeting can improve
  • Visibility — both teams should be able to see where leads are in the lifecycle and what's moving — or not moving — through it

The Payoff

When a lead lifecycle is properly designed and consistently followed, conversion improves at every stage. Leads don't fall through the cracks. Sales spends time on leads that are actually worth pursuing. Marketing gets feedback that improves future campaigns. And both teams have a shared view of pipeline health.

It takes more upfront work than a simple form-to-CRM integration. But it's the difference between a funnel that leaks and one that actually converts.
